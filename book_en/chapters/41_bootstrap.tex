\section{Inference by Resampling}

The analytical standard errors derived from asymptotic theory generally assume
that residuals follow a specific distribution (normal, homoskedastic, i.i.d.).
Efron's (1979) \textbf{bootstrap} replaces distributional assumptions with
resampling: it replicates the estimation process $B$ times on samples drawn
with replacement from the data itself.

\subsection*{Pairs Bootstrap}

The simplest approach resamples pairs $(y_i, x_i)$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item For $b = 1,\ldots,B$: sample $n$ observations with replacement.
  \item Estimate $\hat\theta^{(b)}$ on the resampled dataset.
  \item $\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}_{\mathrm{boot}} = \mathrm{sd}(\hat\theta^{(1)},\ldots,\hat\theta^{(B)})$.
\end{enumerate}

The 95\% bootstrap percentile CI is the interval $[Q_{0{,}025},\, Q_{0{,}975}]$
of the empirical distribution of $\hat\theta^{(b)}$.

\textbf{Advantage}: valid without knowing the distribution of $\hat\theta$ —
robust to non-Gaussian errors, heteroskedasticity, and small samples where the
normal approximation is poor.

\section{DGP Verification}

DGP with uniform error on $[-2, 2]$ — non-Gaussian. With $N = 200$, the
analytical OLS SE may underestimate the true variance.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Comparison of analytical SE vs.\ bootstrap}]
seed(42)
generate df x = rnormal()
generate df e = (rnormal() - 0.5) * 4.0     // U(-2, 2) — non-Gaussian
generate df y = 1.0 + 2.0 * x + e

// standard OLS
let m_ols  = ols(y ~ x, df)
display m_ols

// Bootstrap
let m_boot = bootstrap("ols", y ~ x, df, reps=500)
display m_boot
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection*{Analytical OLS}

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}, basicstyle=\ttfamily\small,
  frame=none, numbers=none, backgroundcolor=\color{codbg},
  xleftmargin=0pt, xrightmargin=0pt, breaklines=false]
OLS: R²=0.178
  const=1.046  SE=0.157   x=1.828  SE=0.279
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection*{Bootstrap ($B = 500$)}

\begin{lstlisting}[language={}, basicstyle=\ttfamily\small,
  frame=none, numbers=none, backgroundcolor=\color{codbg},
  xleftmargin=0pt, xrightmargin=0pt, breaklines=false]
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
                   Bootstrap SE — ols (n=500/500)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Variable    β̂       SE orig.    SE boot    CI lower    CI upper
const    1.0462     0.1566     0.1545     0.7235      1.3710
x        1.8276     0.2787     0.2679     1.3313      2.3910
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\end{lstlisting}

Bootstrap SE ($0{,}155$ and $0{,}268$) and analytical ($0{,}157$ and $0{,}279$)
are similar here, indicating that the analytical OLS SEs are adequate even with
uniform errors when $N = 200$. The agreement validates the implementation:
both converge to the same asymptotic standard deviation.

\section{Syntax}

\begin{lstlisting}
// generic bootstrap — any implemented estimator
bootstrap("ols",    y ~ x, df, reps=500)
bootstrap("logit",  y ~ x, df, reps=500)
bootstrap("qreg",   y ~ x, df, reps=500, q=0.5)

// alias bootse
bootse(y ~ x, df, reps=500)                 // ols by default (legacy)
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Option} & \textbf{Default} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
\texttt{reps} / \texttt{n}  & 1000 & number of bootstrap replicas \\
\texttt{alpha}               & 0.05 & level for CIs (two-tailed) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{nota}
  The bootstrap estimator can also estimate SEs for PSM (ch.~\ref{cap:psm}),
  where the asymptotic distribution of the ATT is non-standard. In this context,
  the pairs bootstrap is consistent (Abadie and Imbens 2008).
\end{nota}
